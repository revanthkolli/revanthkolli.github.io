If you've tried almost every productivity app and technique out there, then you're probably like me when it comes to task management. I was born in 1995 and grew up right alongside the rise of modern tech. Starting in middle school, I would obsessively follow Lifehacker's productivity posts and eagerly jump from one productivity application to another. I've tried GTD, pomodoro, simple lists, bullet journaling, Eisenhower matrix and more. 

Over the years, I've realized that none of these systems work well for me when I try to follow them to the T. Instead, I've formed my own system that takes the parts of each of these systems that I liked and creates something that's simple and really intuitive to the way I think. 

I'm going to try to explain my system below. This is by no means a suggestion that this system is drastically different from others or is a golden goose that will work for everyone (check my disclaimer about this blog in my [About Me](https://writtenbyrevanth.com/about/) for more on why I write these posts). It's merely an attempt at solidifying my own understanding of how I'm currently working.

# Three Pillars

## Inbox

An inbox is important for any productivity setup and is found in many methods, like GTD. The main idea is to be able to quickly add things that you know you want to come back to later. In my implementation, the inbox is mainly for anything that doesn't have a due date. If I can't decide if it has a due date yet, it goes into the inbox. 

I routinely check my inbox to see if there is anything I can work on there or if I can add a due date. Being able to see how long an item has been stuck in my inbox is also important. If something is there too long, it's either because it's really not that important to me or because I've neglected some part of my life. If it's the former I can just archive the item without thinking about it again. If it's the latter, I can revisit it and see why I haven't been able to get to that particular task. Often the solution there is to reframe how I approach that item of break it down into smaller, more actionable pieces.

## Schedule

A calendar is a surprisingly tricky thing in a productivity setup. Calendars exclusively focus on due dates. This makes it extremely easy to prioritize urgent tasks over important ones, just because they show up ahead of the other tasks. What's worse, calendars don't have scope for items that don't have due dates (see Inbox above). In my setup a calendar is extremely helpful, but not what I look at throughout the day. Like my Inbox, I look at my calendar routinely in the morning to see when things are due. It's used as a reference to easily see due dates, but not elevated to the position of absolute control over what I do.

## Focus

My Focus page is what I look at most often throughout a day. It's a subset of my tasks that I have decided are what are important for that particular day. Every morning, I go through my Inbox to check for important tasks that might or might not be urgent and go through my Schedule to look for tasks that are due soon. Looking at both gives me a holistic view of my tasks, making it easy to make smart decisions on what to work on that day. 

Anyone familiar with the Eisenhower Matrix will understand the importance of this part of the system. The Eisenhower Matrix says that there are two attributes that should be considered when prioritizing tasks: Importance and Urgency.

- Not Important & Not Urgent: These tasks should obviously be ignored or delegated. They are not worth the time or effort to complete.
- Urgent: These tasks are what most people and productivity apps tend to focus on. They have due dates and people most often tend to work based off of due dates.
- Important & Not Urgent: These tasks are most often overlooked and constantly put off in favor of completing Urgent tasks. Yet, they are literally important. One of my biggest struggles over the years has been to figure out a system that can help bring focus to these tasks and make them more actionable.

As you can imagine, the Focus part of my system helps make sure that I'm spending some time working on tasks that fall into that last category: Important & Not Urgent. Making sure I spend time on these tasks helps me be more consistent with long-term planning. In addition, working on Important tasks that are not currently Urgent can have the added benefit of preventing an increase in future urgent tasks. For example, scheduling time for gym has an added benefit of keeping my body in shape to avoid future health risks that could arise from not working out.

# Tasks

## Status & Waiting

The tasks in my Focus page are split into a Kanban board based on their status. I use the 4 following statuses: 

- Not Started: Tasks that I haven't started work on
- In Progress: Tasks that I have started and am continuing work on
- Completed: Tasks that I have completed that no longer need work
- Waiting: Tasks that I want to focus on that are not currently actionable because I am waiting on an external factor or because it is not the right time to work on that task

Most apps, like todo apps, just have two easy to see states (Not Started & Completed). Having an In Progress status helps me zoom in from the tasks that are important for the day to the tasks that are most important at that moment. The Waiting status makes sure that my In Progress status isn't being overused (thereby rendering it useless), by taking aside tasks that are not currently actionable. Adding reminders to these tasks helps make sure they're not forgotten and can be moved back to In Progress at the right time.

## Sub Tasks

Sub tasks are simple checkbox tasks that are included in tasks that need to be broken up. For example, when I create a task for a Jira work item that I assign myself to, I create a set of simple sub tasks that help me break down that task in a way that makes sense to me. These sub tasks make the main tasks less daunting and way more actionable. They make it easier to tell how close I am to completing that task at a glance. They also make sure that I don't forget any steps that might not be detailed in or might not make sense to add to the Jira ticket.

## Updates

Updates are dated comments that I use to keep track of peripheral information for larger tasks. They can detail specific actions I took, what road blocks I came across and how they were cleared or even have a more elaborate explanation on why that task is in Waiting. 

I might do a separate blog post and practices I've picked up that have helped me a lot since I started working as a full-time software engineer, but this is definitely one of those. Typing updates makes it a lot easier to give status updates and also jog my own memory if someone comes weeks or months after I've moved on and asks a specific question about that task. I've even found them helpful when coming across bugs related to those tasks after they were completed.

# The Rest

## Big Picture

Big Picture is my system for keeping track of higher level goals. These might be broken down into tasks that can be put into my task management system detailed above or converted into habits (more on them below). I have a separate notes section where I keep my Big Picture notes. At a high level, Big Picture notes are where I pick four or five high level goals to focus on for a given quarter. I then revisit these goals every month to write status updates and keep track of progress. 

I used to do Big Picture tasks for an entire year, but I've learned that that makes the list longer and also harder to complete. So many things change over the course of a year, so reducing the time frame to three months helps me focus on less things at a time but also increase the likelihood of actually achieving the goals.

## Apps

Just during the past year I've extensively used Workflowy, Dynalist, Google Keep, Trello, Evernote, Notion, Roam Research, Amplenote and Todoist at one point or another. I've also briefly explored a plethora of other smaller players in the field, like Moo.do, Joplin and Obsidian. I'm *currently* using Notion as my primary productivity app, along with Google calendar for events or recurring tasks and Hey to keep track of my emails. I imagine my app setup might continue to change as I have a natural curiosity towards how note taking and productivity apps evolve over time. It's been an exciting few years for this field and I'm planning on writing a more in-depth blog post later about my current assessment of the note taking apps that I've used in the past year. 

## Habits

Habits are slightly different from tasks, which is why I don't include them in the rest of the productivity system. They are a way to keep track of some long term goals, but habits by themselves tend to tie closely with routines and routines are hard to manage along with other tasks. I've found there's no current productivity app that can do all of the above and manage habits really well, so I currently use a dedicated habit tracking app (Loop Habit Tracker on Android) to help get a clear picture of how I'm doing.
