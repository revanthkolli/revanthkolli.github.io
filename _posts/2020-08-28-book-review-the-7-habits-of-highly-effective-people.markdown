I remember first seeing this book on a manager's bookshelf during a sultry summer workday in 2012. I was still in high school then. My reading pace had already slowed down in the face of academic rigor and the pressure to get into a good college. I remember wondering if the book was only there for display. 

A year later as I was heading off to college, my dad recommended the same book to me. I added it to my list but soon forgot about it amidst the energized and chaotic hustle of my first year.

Now, I've finally finished reading *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* under circumstances that I couldn't have imagined 8 years ago - stuck indoors while the world is fighting to gain control of a global pandemic.

# TLDR

A 3-star read about creating core habits that can help you be more effective with independent and interdependent conflicts.

# The Habits

#### Independence 

1. **Personal Vision**: Focus on circle of influence (what you can control) instead of circle of concern ("If only they did this...")
2. **Personal Leadership**: Define yourself and your goals in a personal manifesto
3. **Personal Management**: Focus more on important tasks rather than urgent ones (i.e. Eisenhower matrix)

#### Interdependence 

4. **Interpersonal Leadership**: Think win-win and subscribe to an abundance mentality (there's enough potential for everyone to be successful)
5. **Empathic Communication**: Listen without judgement or interruption and show that you understand what the other person is saying before thinking about getting them to understand you
6. **Creative Cooperation**: Aim for synergy, where everyone is on the same side and the goal is to find the best way forward all parties involved
7. **Balanced Self-Renewal**: Focusing on physical (exercise, health), spiritual (clarity, values, focus), mental (life long learning, planning) and social / emotional (service, empathy) self-renewal will help improve the previous six habits

# Notable Takeaways

#### Importance of Self Control

> "Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom." \- Victor Frankl

I'm grateful to Covey for introducing me to this fascinating quote. Victor Frankl was a psychiatrist who survived the Holocaust. He then went on to become a successful professor. His quote is the most succinct and powerful way of highlighting the importance of self-control to success. His book, *Man's Search for Meaning*, now has a spot in my to-read list.

#### Important Becomes Urgent When Ignored

Prioritization isn't new to me and I was familiar with the Eisenhower matrix before reading this book. However, through the anecdotes and repetition in this book, I realized why this is so important. Especially if you're new to planning, it can seem like there are so many urgent tasks that the important ones can always be postponed. Yet, if we don't allocate some time to tackle the important, but not yet urgent, tasks the cycle will never be broken. To get ahead of the cycle and really gain control of your life, you have to schedule in those long-term goals as well.

#### Personal, Not Only Professional

I initially expected this book to be all about professional habits for the workplace. I was pleasantly surprised with the number of personal anecdotes Covey provided about how these habits can help improve family and friendship conflicts as well.  

In the frenzy of post-college work and life, I sometimes forget that we need to continually invest in our personal relationships with family and friends. This is especially true when they are far away and you only get to see them a couple of times a year. Covey's examples helped me remember this fact once again. I've made some adjustments to my goal tracking system to make sure I give enough active attention to these relationships as well as my other priorities.

#### It's All Personal Banking

I'm not sure if Covey came up with this metaphor, but I love it. Reframing social interactions as bank transactions was both exciting and surprisingly intuitive.

Every time we spend effective time with our loved ones, we are essentially depositing emotional currency into their account. Effective here means actually applying the 7 habits to make sure that both parties see that activity as time well-spent. Over time, these deposits add up, creating more fulfilling relationships. When a conflict does occur that cannot lead to a win-win solution, no deal or win-lose options can be accepted more easily without the risk of overdrawing the account.

#### Life Long Learning

Covey's last argument emphasizes the importance of life long learning. There's no such thing as mastering the 7 habits because we all make mistakes and life is hard. Therefore, the goal isn't to master them, but to continually try to get better. According to Covey, the habits should be viewed as a spiral with levels. As we get better at practicing them we reach higher levels of implementation which are even harder and will take more work. I'm glad Covey focuses on this aspect of self-improvement because it's easy to subscribe to the idea that there's an end goal or that the end goal has already been reached. There have been many times where I thought I mastered a certain skill or moved past a specific flaw only to realize that there was still more work ahead. Staying grounded and understanding your shortcomings is a life long journey, but it can be all the more rewarding.

# In Summary

*7 Habits* is a solid read and covers some very fundamental truths about how to be successful. Modern success is dependent on effective interpersonal communication and relationships. That effectiveness has to come from within with clarity and intention. While some parts of the book were lengthy and others were not as precise as they could have been, especially in the second half of the book, I still really enjoyed it. Perhaps the best indicator that this was worth my time is the fact that I think about the central ideas from this book almost weekly and have modified my personal accountability system with these habits in mind.
